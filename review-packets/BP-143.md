## Summary

BP-143 captures the One-Shot Write Permit architecture as a pure design document.
It replaces the three standing write env-flags (which leave permission open as a
long-lived state) with per-act, single-use, time-limited permits.

No code, no register, no corridor change, no flag runtime change. The build is a
separate contract: BP-144.

## Why design-only, and why BP-144 separately

Phase B went live on 2026-06-01: the first real autonomous sandbox write
succeeded (commit f95fa11 in G-Dislioglu/bluepilot-sandbox). Building the
permission system in the same breath, minutes after the first write, would be the
fatigue trap. The clean step is to fix the design first, then build.

The build is deliberately a two-service change and is left to BP-144:
- maya-core: permit register plus atomic consume (compare-and-set) plus
  expiry/revoke. The current assessCorridor does not yet know the permit fields
  (permitId, target repo, path, op, expectedBaseSha, contentHash); it must check
  them instead of hanging on the env-flag.
- bluepilot-builder: the permit fields must be threaded from the task through the
  corridor and into opusSmartPush; the final hash check sits at the byte-exit of
  opusSmartPush/putFileContent.

## The two hard requirements (Codex), non-negotiable for BP-144

1. Content binding: the permit must bind to a content-hash of the concrete write,
   not just repo + file. Otherwise any content could be written to the named file
   between issue and execution.
2. Atomic single-use: consumption must be one server-side step that checks
   "valid?" and sets "consumed" together (compare-and-set), so a second concurrent
   write goes empty.

## Four further same-risk-class decisions added (BP-143 design)

A. The hash is recomputed at the builder byte-exit, from the exact bytes sent to
   GitHub. Binding only at issue-time would move the window, not close it.
B. The permit fixes create vs. update and carries the expected base-sha; GitHub's
   PUT then rejects a changed file, acting as a second atomic guard.
C. A policy-independent protected-path floor: a machine-issued permit may only
   ever target bluepilot-sandbox; soulmatch and maya-core are human-issued only.
D. One permit = one attempt; a failed write needs a fresh permit.

## Result

BP-143 is a design/documentation task only. It leaves the runtime untouched and
hands BP-144 a complete, pressure-tested target: separate capability/permission/
policy, bind the permit to the exact write, consume atomically, recompute the hash
at the byte-exit, keep soulmatch human-only by a floor that policy cannot relax,
and keep MAYA_BUILDER_REPO_WRITE_ENABLED as a dumb fail-closed kill-switch.
